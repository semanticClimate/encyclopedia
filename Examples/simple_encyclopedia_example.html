Climate Change
Also known as: Global Warming, Climate Crisis
Wikidata ID: Q7942
Category: Environmental Issue
Wikipedia: Climate change

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns. While climate change is a natural phenomenon, scientific evidence shows that human activities since the mid-20th century have accelerated these changes. The primary driver is the increased emission of greenhouse gases from burning fossil fuels, deforestation, and industrial processes.

Global Warming
Also known as: Climate Change, Climate Crisis
Wikidata ID: Q7942 (merged with Climate Change)
Category: Environmental Issue
Wikipedia: Global warming

Global warming is the long-term heating of Earth's climate system observed since the pre-industrial period due to human activities, primarily fossil fuel burning, which increases heat-trapping greenhouse gas levels in Earth's atmosphere.

Greenhouse Gas
Wikidata ID: Q13138
Category: Atmospheric Chemistry
Wikipedia: Greenhouse gas

A greenhouse gas is a gas that absorbs and emits radiant energy within the thermal infrared range, causing the greenhouse effect. The primary greenhouse gases in Earth's atmosphere are water vapor, carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and ozone.

Carbon Dioxide
Also known as: CO₂, Carbon(IV) oxide
Wikidata ID: Q1218
Category: Chemical Compound
Wikipedia: Carbon dioxide

Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is a chemical compound made up of molecules that each have one carbon atom covalently double bonded to two oxygen atoms. It is found in the gas state at room temperature and is a major greenhouse gas contributing to climate change.

CO₂
Also known as: Carbon Dioxide, Carbon(IV) oxide
Wikidata ID: Q1218 (merged with Carbon Dioxide)
Category: Chemical Compound
Wikipedia: Carbon dioxide

CO₂ is the chemical formula for carbon dioxide, a colorless gas with a density about 53% higher than that of dry air. It is produced by respiration and combustion of fossil fuels.

Fossil Fuels
Wikidata ID: Q188425
Category: Energy Source
Wikipedia: Fossil fuel

Fossil fuels are hydrocarbon-containing materials such as coal, oil, and natural gas, formed naturally in the Earth's crust from the remains of dead plants and animals that are extracted and burned as a fuel. When burned, they release large amounts of carbon dioxide, a greenhouse gas that contributes to climate change.

IPCC
Wikidata ID: Not available
Wikipedia: Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) is a United Nations body for assessing the science related to climate change. It provides policymakers with regular scientific assessments on climate change, its implications and potential future risks.

Greenhouse Effect
Wikidata ID: Q193078
Category: Atmospheric Phenomenon
Wikipedia: Greenhouse effect

The greenhouse effect is the process by which radiation from a planet's atmosphere warms the planet's surface to a temperature above what it would be without this atmosphere. Greenhouse gases in the atmosphere trap heat, contributing to global warming.

Methane
Wikidata ID: Q1490
Category: Chemical Compound
Wikipedia: Methane

Methane (CH₄) is a chemical compound with the chemical formula CH₄. It is a group-14 hydride and the simplest alkane, and is the main constituent of natural gas. Methane is a potent greenhouse gas, with a global warming potential much higher than carbon dioxide over a 20-year period.

Renewable Energy
Wikidata ID: Q188836
Category: Energy Technology
Wikipedia: Renewable energy

Renewable energy is energy from sources that are naturally replenishing but flow-limited. Renewable resources are virtually inexhaustible in duration but limited in the amount of energy that is available per unit of time. Examples include solar, wind, and hydroelectric power. Transitioning to renewable energy is crucial for mitigating climate change and reducing dependence on fossil fuels.
